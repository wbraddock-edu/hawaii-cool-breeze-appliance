How Humidity Affects Your Appliances in Hawaii

By Cool Breeze Team · June 14, 2024 · 6 min read

Hawaii's humidity is one of the things we love about living here — but it's also one of the biggest enemies of household appliances. The Big Island's lush, wet environment creates conditions that accelerate wear, corrosion, and mechanical failure in ways that mainland homeowners rarely have to deal with. Understanding how humidity affects your appliances can help you extend their life and avoid costly breakdowns.

How Humidity Damages Appliances

Moisture in the air accelerates the oxidation of metal components. Control boards, wiring connectors, motor contacts, and relay switches are all vulnerable to corrosion from humidity. When metal corrodes, it creates resistance in electrical connections, leading to overheating, intermittent operation, and eventually failure. This is why appliances on Hawaii Island often fail years earlier than their mainland counterparts — the same model in Denver might last 15 years; in Hilo it might last 10.

Refrigerators and Freezers

Refrigerators work harder in humid, warm environments. The compressor runs more frequently to maintain temperature as warm, moist air enters every time the door is opened. This additional runtime accelerates compressor wear. Condenser coils can accumulate mold and bacterial growth in Hawaii's humidity, reducing heat exchange efficiency. Clean your condenser coils every 6 months in Hawaii — more often than the annual cleaning recommended for dry climates.

Washers and Dryers

Front-load washers are particularly susceptible to mold and mildew growth in Hawaii's humidity. The rubber door gasket and drum interior can harbor mold even with regular use. After each wash, leave the door ajar to allow the interior to dry. Run a monthly cleaning cycle with a washing machine cleaner. Dryers should always be vented to the outside — in a humid environment, an unvented dryer just recirculates moisture back into your laundry room and home.

Ovens and Ranges

Gas igniter systems are particularly vulnerable to moisture. The ceramic igniters can crack when heated repeatedly after being exposed to high humidity, causing clicking or failure to light. Electronic control panels on modern ranges can develop intermittent faults from humidity-related oxidation on circuit board contacts. Keeping your kitchen well-ventilated reduces moisture buildup around your range.

Prevention Strategies

Several strategies help protect appliances from Hawaii's humidity. Using dehumidifiers in enclosed laundry rooms and garages where appliances are located can significantly reduce moisture exposure. Ensuring good air circulation around appliances — particularly the condenser coils at the back or bottom of refrigerators — improves efficiency and reduces moisture accumulation. Silica gel packets placed inside appliance storage compartments can absorb excess moisture. And most importantly, addressing minor issues promptly before humidity-related corrosion spreads further into the appliance's components.
