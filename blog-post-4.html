When to Repair vs. Replace an Appliance in Hawaii

By Cool Breeze Team · April 28, 2024 · 6 min read

When an appliance breaks down, one of the first questions homeowners ask is: should I repair it or replace it? In Hawaii, this question has some unique dimensions. Appliance prices are higher due to shipping costs, delivery and installation on the islands involves additional logistics, and Hawaii's climate means even new appliances face accelerated wear. Here's a framework for making the right decision.

The 50% Rule

A common rule of thumb in appliance repair: if the repair cost exceeds 50% of the cost of a new appliance, and the appliance is more than halfway through its expected lifespan, replacement may be more economical. However, in Hawaii this calculus shifts toward repair more often than on the mainland. A new mid-range refrigerator that costs $1,200 on Amazon costs $1,500-$1,800 delivered and installed on Hawaii Island. If the repair is $600, that's well under 50% of the true replacement cost.

Consider the Age and Expected Lifespan

Typical appliance lifespans in dry climates: refrigerators 15 years, washers and dryers 10-13 years, dishwashers 9-12 years, ovens and ranges 13-15 years. In Hawaii's tropical climate, subtract 2-3 years from each figure. An 8-year-old washer that needs a $300 repair has potentially 4+ years of life left — that repair often makes financial sense. A 12-year-old refrigerator with a failing compressor requiring $600-$800 in parts and labor is closer to replacement territory.

Energy Efficiency Considerations

Older appliances are significantly less energy efficient than modern ones. Hawaii has among the highest electricity rates in the nation — currently around $0.38-$0.42 per kWh on Oahu and Hawaii Island. A 15-year-old refrigerator using 900 kWh/year costs about $360/year in electricity. A modern ENERGY STAR model using 450 kWh/year costs $180/year — a $180/year savings. When calculating repair vs. replace, factor in this energy cost differential. Sometimes replacing an old but functional appliance simply for energy savings makes financial sense over a 5-year horizon.

Repair History Matters

An appliance that has needed multiple repairs in recent years is showing signs of end-of-life failure. If you've spent $400 in repairs in the past two years and it needs another $300 repair, that $700 total approaches replacement cost. Track your repair history — it's a key input in this decision. Most homeowners forget what they spent on previous repairs and underestimate the cumulative cost.

When Replacement Almost Always Makes Sense

Some scenarios where replacement is usually the better choice: the appliance is out of warranty and the main component (compressor, motor, control board) has failed and costs more than $500 to replace; the appliance is over 15 years old; parts are no longer available for your model; or a newer model would provide significantly better functionality that matters to you. In these cases, repair is throwing good money after bad.
